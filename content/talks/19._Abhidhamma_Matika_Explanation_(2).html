19. Abhidhamma Matika Explanation (2)

By Phra Ajahn Funn Ācāro

Wat Paa Udomsomphorn, Sakon Nakorn, Thailand

9. Nevavipākanavipākadhammadhammā

Next, I shall explain the ninth section: nevavipākanavipākadhammadhammā. The meaning: states that are neither result nor cause — states without result and without cause. Explanation: states that have neither cause nor effect. Like a person who dreams that he has eaten his fill of delicious food. When he wakes up, the food and the fullness in the dream disappear completely. The food is the cause, the fullness is the result, and the dreaming person is the state called nevavipākanavipākadhammadhammā. That is Nibbāna itself. As long as one has not yet seen Nibbāna, that state depends on the merit and demerit one creates. But upon reaching Nibbāna, all merit and demerit that were created disappear completely, as the Pali states: esa dhammo sanantano — This alone is the ancient Dhamma, the Dhamma that delights the virtuous ones headed by the Buddha. Or another translation: it is the Dhamma of the noble ones who have crossed beyond defilements.

The Sakkavadi Teacher then asked: "This single Dhamma belonging to the noble ones headed by the Buddha — I have been doubtful about this for a long time. I want to know and understand it easily. Therefore, please explain it so that I may settle my understanding now."

The Paravadi Teacher gave a simile: This Dhamma is like a person dyeing cloth with various colors such as yellow, red, black, etc. The person who dyes the cloth represents conditioned phenomena (saṅkhāra). The various colors represent result states (vipāka dhamma), i.e., the fruits of merit and demerit. The cloth itself represents the state called nevavipākanavipākadhammadhammā. When the person dyes the cloth and the various colors disappear from it, the cloth returns to its original pure white color. Wise people with discernment should infer and understand according to the profound and detailed words of the Buddha as presented briefly in this ninth matika section.

10. Upādinnupādāniyā Dhammā

Next, I shall explain the tenth tika matika according to the Pali: upādinnupādāniyā dhammā. Meaning: states that sustain the nature which is the basis for grasping (upādāna) — the act of clinging to form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness as permanent and stable, deludedly considering them good and beautiful, holding them as truly one's own. Therefore it is called upādāna. It can only arise because of not knowing them as danger, as value, as beneficial to oneself. Hence one becomes deluded, attached, and pleased. But in truth, these forms, feelings, perceptions, formations, consciousness give many kinds of danger. The danger of form, for example: cold is suffering, heat is suffering, hunger and thirst are suffering.

To make it clear with a simile: one who holds form as his own is like a person who sets a bushfire hoping to catch a turtle. When he encounters a venomous snake, he mistakes it for a fish, reaches in and grabs the snake. The snake bites him and he dies. Likewise, one who holds form as his own is deluded into doing only evil kamma, nurturing his own form and the forms of others. After death, he experiences only suffering in the four lower realms including hell, precisely because of clinging to form as his own.

Another simile: like a person who dies from a venomous snake bite. Furthermore, one who holds form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness as his own — but they are not his own because they are changeable (pariṇāmadhamma), subject to transformation. Yet he still clings to them as his own. Therefore he desires only happiness and comfort, while form, feeling, etc., constantly change. He nevertheless clings to them as his own. Hence he encounters many kinds of difficulty and mental distress because of upādāna (clinging) that grasps and does not let go.

11. Anupādinnupādāniyā Dhammā

In the eleventh section: anupādinnupādāniyā dhammā. Meaning: states that are the basis for grasping objects that should not be clung to. Explanation: The eight worldly conditions (lokadhamma): gain, status, praise, happiness — these four are desirable objects (iṭṭhārammaṇa); loss of gain, loss of status, blame, suffering — these four are undesirable objects (aniṭṭhārammaṇa). These eight are called worldly conditions because they cause the mind to become clouded and disturbed; they are not calming, and they cause various mental agitations. Therefore the Blessed Buddha declared: anupādinnupādāniyā dhammā — states that should not be grasped as objects. Because these eight worldly conditions agitate the minds of all beings. Explanation: these eight worldly conditions are like a razor-sharp wheel that spins beings around to experience various sufferings, like the peta (hungry ghost) who desires the wheel to cut off his head. Yet all beings see these eight worldly conditions as supreme happiness, just as the eight worldly conditions cause beings to be disturbed and agitated, experiencing only various sufferings.

12. Anupādinnaanupādāniyā Dhammā

In the twelfth section: anupādinnaanupādāniyā dhammā. Meaning: states that are the basis for determining which states should be clung to and which should not. Explanation: The states that should be clung to are the Noble Eightfold Path mentioned earlier. The meaning: not clinging to the eight worldly conditions, but clinging to the Noble Eightfold Path. Therefore it is called anupādinnaanupādāniyā dhammā. The explanation of the twelfth tika matika concludes here.

13. Saṃkiliṭṭhasaṃkilesikā Dhammā

Next, I shall explain the thirteenth section according to the Pali: saṃkiliṭṭhasaṃkilesikā dhammā. Meaning: states that are defiled and defiling to the mind. Explanation: Some people think about those who have insulted them, killed them, defeated them, stolen from them. These four conditions are entirely defiling to the mind. Like a grasshopper that delights in a flame, thinking it is something good. When it flies into the flame, the fire burns its wings and tail, and it is about to die. Then it realizes it is suffering. Therefore the Pali says saṃkiliṭṭhasaṃkilesikā dhammā. The mind was already defiled, and then one further thinks of defiled objects, mixing them with the already defiled mind, causing the mind to become even more defiled.

14. Asaṃkiliṭṭhasaṃkilesikā Dhammā

In the fourteenth matika section, according to the Pali: asaṃkiliṭṭhasaṃkilesikā dhammā. Meaning: states associated with a defiled mind that thinks only of undefiled objects. Explanation: Some people whose mind is already defiled, but then think of the virtues of the Triple Gem: the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha. Whoever reveres these Three Gems can achieve whatever they wish. Simile: like a person swimming who thinks only of his morality, generosity, and wholesome deeds as objects. The example of Suppabuddha: Suppabuddha was originally poor and destitute, afflicted with many diseases, enduring unbearable hardship. But he constantly reflected on the virtues of the Buddha, Dhamma, and Saṅgha as objects. Later, he enjoyed great wealth, and all his diseases disappeared. Therefore it accords with the Pali: asaṃkiliṭṭhasaṃkilesikā dhammā.

15. Asaṃkiliṭṭhaasaṃkilesikā Dhammā

In the fifteenth section, according to the Pali: asaṃkiliṭṭhaasaṃkilesikā dhammā. Meaning: states that sustain an undefiled mind, and the thinking is only of undefiled states. Explanation: The mind is accompanied by states that remove the defiling states from one's character. Like an infant who takes no delight in sense pleasures; removing them is to eliminate sense pleasures. Therefore it accords with the Pali: asaṃkiliṭṭhaasaṃkilesikā dhammā, meaning that the states are not defiled, the mind is not defiled, and the thinking of mental objects is not defiled. The explanation of the fifteenth tika matika concludes here.

16. Savitakkasavicārā Dhammā

Next, I shall explain the sixteenth tika matika according to the Pali: savitakkasavicārā dhammā. Meaning: states that occur with both initial application (vitakka) and sustained application (vicāra). Explanation: A person who reflects and thinks various thoughts. Like a stream-enterer (sotāpanna) who considers: 'My identity view, doubt, and clinging to rites and rituals are gone, no longer in my character. How can I avoid wrong thoughts and abandon sensual desire and ill-will?' He thinks: 'Sensual desire...' Such thinking is called vicāra (sustained application). Then he further thinks: 'Sensual desire and ill-will still exist in my character. How can I think in such a way that I abandon sensual desire and ill-will?' The Sakkavadi Teacher requested a simile: "Sir, I still have doubt about this matter. Please give a simile." The Paravadi then gave a simile: Like a merchant investing for profit. The trade is very profitable, but he still worries that others might come and compete. Alternatively, like a lay donor, layman or laywoman, who has the opportunity to offer alms to the Saṅgha headed by the Buddha. They examine and test their own offerings, see they are good, and then think: 'When the monks eat, will they all like it, or will some not like it?' They observe and adjust the sour, sweet, oily, salty, etc. This is called vitakka and vicāra. Therefore it accords with the Pali: savitakkasavicārā dhammā.

17. Avitakkavicāramattā Dhammā

In the seventeenth section: avitakkavicāramattā dhammā. Meaning: states that sustain a mind that has only sustained application (vicāra) without initial application (vitakka). Explanation: A person who performs wholesome good conduct, the mind not wavering or shaking, thinks: 'This is meritorious and wholesome,' then lowers his head and does it, without later regret or suspicion. Like a skilled money-changer who says of a coin, 'good' if it is good, 'bad' if it is bad, because he trusts his own hands and eyes. The Sakkavadi asked: "What you say about a person without vitakka but only vicāra — I am doubtful. If there is no vitakka, where would vicāra come from? Please give a simile for further understanding." The Paravadi then gave a simile: A person with only vicāra, no vitakka, is like a banana blossom. Initially it is a blossom; the banana fruits are not called a blossom, as in common worldly speech. That person does not deliberate about anything; he acts according to his own preference. Therefore it accords with the Pali: avitakkavicāramattā dhammā.

18. Avitakkāvicārā Dhammā

In the eighteenth section: avitakkāvicārā dhammā. Meaning: states that sustain a mind without initial or sustained application. Explanation: A certain type of person acts without deliberation or consideration. When others say it is good, he agrees; he has only faith. The Sakkavadi asked: "Who is an example of a person without vitakka and vicāra, having only faith?" The Paravadi gave an example: When Ānanda was practicing mindfulness of the body (kāyagatāsati kammaṭṭhāna), contemplating the 32 parts in forward and reverse order, he still could not attain the destruction of the taints (āsavakkhaya). Because Ānanda was still thinking about the Blessed Buddha and trusting the prophecy that he would attain the destruction of the taints that day. He thought: 'Why haven't I attained it? Perhaps my effort is too strong. I will rest a little.' As soon as he thought to rest and leaned his body down, his vicāra on the 32 parts ceased, and his vitakka about the Blessed One ceased. He then attained the destruction of the taints, becoming an arahant with analytical knowledge (paṭisambhidā-ñāṇa), because he cut off vitakka and vicāra. Therefore it accords with the Pali: avitakkāvicārā dhammā, meaning states without vitakka and vicāra. Wise people with discernment should infer and understand according to the profound and detailed words of the Buddha. This concludes the brief explanation of the eighteenth tika matika.

19. Pītisahagatā Dhammā

Now I shall explain the nineteenth matika section in continuation according to the Pali: pītisahagatā dhammā. Meaning: states accompanied by rapture (pīti). Rapture has five characteristics: momentary rapture (khaṇikā pīti), minor rapture (khuddakā pīti), overwhelming rapture (okkanṭikā pīti), uplifting rapture (ubbeṅgā pīti), and suffusing rapture (pharaṇā pīti). Momentary rapture arises momentarily; when it arises, it is like a flash of lightning. Minor rapture arises slightly; when it arises, it is like waves lapping the shore. Overwhelming rapture, when it arises, causes the body to tremble. Uplifting rapture, when it arises, causes hairs to stand on end and the body to float in the air. Suffusing rapture, when it arises, causes a thrilling sensation throughout the entire body, like a person who has consumed delicious food. Rapture (pīti) means satisfaction, delighting beings in this mundane sphere. The Sakkavadi asked: "Rapture means satisfaction. A person who eats food is satisfied; gaining desired wealth is satisfaction; a scattered mind is satisfied; a tranquil mind is satisfied — all are called pīti. What is pīti satisfaction in this context?" The Paravadi explained: Pīti here means satisfaction because there is no further concern. Like a person who is very hungry goes looking for food according to his preference. When he has eaten and is full, his concern ends; he does not need to seek further. Like Vakkali Bhikkhu, who intended to satisfy his heart by looking at the Buddha's form and features. When he did not see the Buddha, he was sad and depressed. When he saw the Buddha, his sadness disappeared. This satisfaction is called pīti. Therefore it accords with the Pali: pītisahagatā dhammā.

20. Sukhasahagatā Dhammā

In the twentieth section: sukhasahagatā dhammā. Meaning: states accompanied by happiness (sukha). Happiness is of two types: bodily happiness — freedom from affliction by disease; and mental happiness — freedom from defilement, happiness that accords with one's thoughts and actions. There are also four types of happiness: happiness from long life, happiness from beauty, happiness from physical strength, wealth, and wisdom, happiness from being free from enemies and kamma. Briefly, there are two types: mundane happiness (lokiya sukha) and supramundane happiness (lokuttara sukha).

Explanation: freedom from affliction by disease is called comfort; comfort is called happiness. Happiness accompanied by defilements is called mundane happiness. Happiness without defilements is called supramundane happiness. As an example of these two types of happiness: The novice Sukha was originally poor and destitute. He offered a gift to a paccekabuddha and from then on was happy, continuing until the time of the Buddha Gotama. This is the sphere of mundane happiness. From the day he attained arahantship, cutting off defilements completely, that is the sphere of supramundane happiness.

Sukho viveko — seclusion from defilements is a kind of happiness. Sukho buddhānamuppādo — the arising of Buddhas in the world is a kind of happiness. Sukhā dhammadessenā — listening to the true Dhamma teaching of the Buddha is a kind of happiness. Sukhā saṅghassa sāmaggī — the harmony of the Saṅgha is a kind of happiness. Tapo sukho — the burning of defilements, i.e., gradually weakening them from one's character, is a kind of happiness. Conducting oneself with physical seclusion (kāya-viveka), seclusion from acquisitions (upadhi-viveka), and mental seclusion (citta-viveka) — these three kinds of seclusion are called happiness. Therefore it accords with the Pali: sukhasahagatā dhammā. This concludes the brief explanation of the twentieth matika section.

21. Upekkhāsahagatā Dhammā

In the twenty-first section: upekkhāsahagatā dhammā. Meaning: states that are the objects of an equanimous mind — devoid of rapture, devoid of happiness, thus classified as equanimity (upekkhā). Here, equanimity arises from happiness, and happiness arises from rapture. Only when happiness and rapture cease does equanimity arise. Explanation: without rapture and happiness, equanimity arises. If rapture and happiness still exist, equanimity cannot arise. Equanimity here means that rapture and happiness have arisen and then disappeared. As long as rapture and happiness still exist, it is not equanimity. If rapture and happiness never existed, equanimity would not exist either, because equanimity cannot arise without them. Like light arising from a flame. A flame can arise only depending on fuel and tinder. The tinder is rapture, the flame is happiness, and the light is equanimity.

Therefore the Blessed One taught: rapture, happiness, equanimity in sequence. Like three kinds of happiness: human happiness, heavenly happiness, and Nibbāna happiness. Human happiness is not as great as heavenly happiness; heavenly happiness is not as great as Nibbāna happiness. The story of King Mahājambupati is an example. Initially, Mahājambupati enjoyed the happiness of royal sovereignty as a human, but was not content; he saw heavenly happiness as superior. When he saw the happiness of Nibbāna, which is supreme compared to both, he abandoned both those kinds of happiness. These three kinds of happiness — human, heavenly, Nibbāna — correspond to the three states: rapture, happiness, equanimity. Therefore it accords with the Pali: upekkhāsahagatā dhammā. This concludes the explanation of the twenty-first matika section.

22. Dassanena Pahātabbā Dhammā

Next, I shall explain the twenty-second tika matika according to the Pali: dassanena pahātabbā dhammā. Meaning: states whereby one should abandon defilements because of seeing. Explanation: A mind that scatters after various objects, when it sees, becomes stricken with spiritual urgency and abandons defilements. Like the wise novice who, upon seeing his own hair, generated wisdom, contemplating it as foulness (asubha): 'My hair is not beautiful; others' hair is the same.' Arahantship then arose. The Sakkavadi asked: "Most people see, yet why do we not abandon defilements by seeing? Is there a difference in the nature of seeing?" The Paravadi explained: There is a difference in the nature of seeing. Some seeing gives rise to defilements; some seeing abandons defilements. It depends on what is seen. If one sees something beautiful and pleasing, it gives rise to defilements. Therefore the Blessed Buddha taught the assembly: asubhānupassiṃ viharantaṃ — dwelling in the contemplation of foulness. His intention was to dwell with the perception of un-beautiful objects. Therefore it accords with the Pali: dassanena pahātabbā dhammā.

23. Bhāvanāya Pahātabbā Dhammā

In the twenty-third section: bhāvanāya pahātabbā dhammā. Meaning: states whereby one should abandon defilements through development (bhāvanā). Development is of three types: preparatory development (parikamma bhāvanā) which abandons coarse defilements; access development (upacāra bhāvanā) which abandons medium defilements; attainment development (appanā bhāvanā) which abandons subtle defilements. As examples of those who abandoned defilements and caused special Dhamma to arise through the power of these three developments, there are many. For instance, the monk who saw the teeth of a woman; or Lady Rūpanandā who saw a vision more beautiful than her own form; or the five hundred paccekabuddhas who saw a lotus wither. This is called bhāvanā. Explanation: coarse wisdom abandons coarse defilements; medium wisdom abandons medium defilements; subtle wisdom abandons subtle defilements. Bhāvanā means knowing what is bad and what is good, eliminating evil completely, and causing good to arise within oneself. That is called bhāvanā. Therefore it accords with the Pali: bhāvanāya pahātabbā dhammā.

24. Nevadassanena Na Bhāvanāya Pahātabbā Dhammā

In the twenty-fourth section: nevadassanena na bhāvanāya pahātabbā dhammā. Meaning: states that should be abandoned neither by seeing nor by development. Explanation: States that abandon defilements through natural disposition (nisaya). Like the wise man Dukula, who neither saw nor developed, but simply made his mind not delight in sense pleasures, like a person with a good mind, not greedy, not angry, not deluded, remaining neutral. Therefore it is said to be according to natural disposition. A person who abandons defilements without development, simply by not delighting in sense pleasures like Dukula, is extremely rare. Therefore it accords with the Pali: nevadassanena na bhāvanāya pahātabbā dhammā. This concludes the explanation of the twenty-fourth tika matika.
