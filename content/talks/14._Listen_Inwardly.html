14. Listen Inwardly

By Phra Ajahn Funn Ācāro

Wat Paa Udomsomphorn, Sakon Nakorn, Thailand

July 22, 1975

Just listening to the sermon is not enough; you must also listen to the Dhamma itself for it to be good. A sermon is instruction. What does the teacher teach? He teaches you to know right from wrong, good from bad. Now we must listen to the Dhamma – listen inwardly, listen to rūpa-dhamma and nāma-dhamma. To listen to the Dhamma, you must listen right here. In the past, people listened to the Dhamma and attained the path and fruit. We listen nowadays but don't attain anything because we only listen to the sound. We come here wanting happiness and comfort. So sit comfortably, with dignity, with a bright and cheerful smile.

Once your body is comfortable, reflect on your refuge. What do you take as refuge? You take the Buddha’s virtue, the Dhamma’s virtue, the Saṅgha’s virtue. Bring them to mind. Where is the Buddha? Where is the Dhamma? Where is the Saṅgha? Why is it said that these three are refuges? How can they be relied upon? Where is the Buddha? Bring it to mind – where is that knowing? Buddho, the Buddha, is in the heart. Dhammo, the Dhamma, is in the heart. Saṅgho, the Noble Disciple, is in the heart. Truly in the heart. Consider this: Buddho, Dhammo, Saṅgho – where are you recollecting them? In the heart, isn't that so? This is it. We want to know and see how the Buddha taught us to be freed from suffering. Religion is the tool for remedying suffering, for extinguishing suffering. Where do we remedy suffering? Think about it.

Buddho – that knowing – where is it? Dhammo – what is it that does the work? Where is the Dhamma right now? And Saṅgho – where are the practitioners right now? First recite the meditation word: Buddho, Dhammo, Saṅgho three times. Then focus just on Buddho, Buddho, one word. Close your eyes. Shut your mouth. Don't let your tongue move. Bring it to mind in the heart. Where is that knowing? Establish mindfulness right there. Let your ears listen right there. Let your eyes see right there. See right at that which feels. Fix your mindfulness right there.

We want to know: where do all forms of happiness and suffering come from? You don't need to think about the past or the future. You must determine to see right now. The past has already gone – you don't need to think about what it was like. You cannot know the future. We are ordinary people (puthujjana). We must look right now. The Buddha did not tell us to dwell on the past; he told us to contemplate the present moment. Recite Buddho, Buddho to know the foundation. Wherever that knowing is, establish mindfulness there – that is, at the heart. Focus on Buddho as the one who knows. If the present moment is good, the future will also be good. If the present moment is not good, the future will not be good. Simply put, examine it yourselves.

We want happiness and comfort. As you recite and contemplate, your mind becomes calm and peaceful. It does not run forward or backward, left or right, above or below. It stays fixed right in the middle, at that which knows Buddho. Then the mind is good, peaceful and at ease, cool in heart and mind, without suffering or distress. This brings happiness and prosperity in the present and in the future. So look into it yourselves. You want to be good. Right now it is not good – why is that? Your mind is not good; it is restless and struggling. The mind is not good – it is suffering, chaotic, agitated. This is what leads beings to fall into misery in the present and in the future.

Knowing this, quickly correct it right now. Don't let it pass even a moment. If you leave what is bad in place, then you will remain a bad person. This is it. People blame this and that as bad, doing this is bad, doing that is bad. Why is it bad? We don't look at its origin. We don't look at its root. Where does the root come from? If the root is not good, how can the tip be good? We must look at its root – that is, this heart. This is the root. If the root is good, the fruit will be good. It's like a tree: if you take good care of the root, fertilize and water it well, the tree will be good, producing abundant flowers and fruit. The same here. If our heart is good, then everything we do becomes good. Whatever livelihood we pursue becomes good: trade, government service, family, relatives, neighbors, the country – all good. Whether ordained or not, if the heart is good, there will be no quarrels, no distress, only peace.

This is how the Buddha taught in the past. Those who heard and received his instructions attained the path and fruit – many becoming stream-enterers (sotāpanna), once-returners (sakadāgāmī), non-returners (anāgāmī), and arahants (arahatta). But nowadays, even though we listen, no one is seen to attain the religion. Why? Because we only listen to the sound. We say a good sound is good – but what is actually good? We must make it opanayiko – bring it down into our hearts. You want to be good: is your heart good yet? If the heart is not good, you will not be good.

Simply put, speaking of evil – is your heart still evil? If you know it is evil, not good, then abandon it. Why hold onto what is not good? It's not that the sky or air is not good. It's not that trees, mountains, or fields are not good. It's not that the country or roads are not good. It's the heart of people that is not good. The heart of people is chaotic. The heart of people is distressed. Look: is suffering in possessions, money, and gold? Or is suffering in food, water, and nourishment? Or in clothes and fabrics? Listen now. Those things are not the problem. They don't say they are suffering; they don't say they are happy. It is our heart that suffers. Why does it suffer? Because of delusion. It mistakes itself for a self, for a being. It takes things personally – "he said this about me" – and gets lost in that. It mistakes good for good, bad for bad.

When we contemplate properly, we see that things themselves do not declare themselves good or bad. Ask them: Is gold good or is silver good? Does gold say it is good? Does silver say it is good? Or do they say they are bad? We are the ones who say it. They remain neutral, don't they? If people speak that way, we can't take it. If we don't like it, we get angry, right? If we like it, we become delighted, don't we? This is what you must look at. Contemplate right here. Don't go contemplating anything else. That's what the Buddha taught.

In the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta he said: "Bhikkhus, do not indulge in these two extremes. Walk the middle way." What are the two extremes? They are attraction on one side and aversion on the other. If we cling to either one, we suffer.

Therefore, he does not let us cling to them. He tells us to walk the middle way. It's like walking through a rough path: if you turn to this side, you get thorns; if you turn to that side, you get thorns. If you don't turn off, but walk straight ahead, there is no suffering, isn't that so? Likewise, if our mind gets lost and turns toward attraction but doesn't get what it wants, we suffer. If it turns toward aversion, not wanting to see or hear something, but then sees or hears it anyway, we suffer, don't we? So we must not turn off. Walk the middle way. Whatever arises, know it, but don't cling to it.

Buddho, the knower, is right here in the middle of our chest. From now on, you should all come to know and see this. This is the practice, the conduct that reaches Dhamma, reaches Vinaya, reaches the religion. Know and reach your own body and mind. Right now, we don't reach our body. We don't reach our mind. Our mind floats off after sense objects: forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tactile objects. It feeds on external objects. But when it reaches our body, it sees happiness and suffering. Why is there happiness? Because our mind is calm, still, equanimous – not sending itself forward, backward, left, right, up, or down. It stays fixed in the middle, right at that knowing. This mind is not something that is inherently suffering or difficult. It has been this way from the very beginning. But we mix all sorts of things into it, and so it becomes all sorts of things. Therefore, the Buddha taught us to let go.

This is the practice. You should all know and see the way things are, the fact of their existence. How did those who listened to the Dhamma attain the path and fruit? How did they attain?

I'll give you a simple analogy, like building a hall. Have you finished building the hall? When it's finished, what is it? When it's not yet finished, what is it? It is posts, wood, planks, corrugated iron, nails, various things. At that stage, we don't call it a hall. Then a skilled carpenter assembles them, hammers them together into a single mass. All those separate things disappear. There are no longer any posts, beams, or rafters – unless you go looking for them individually, you won't see them. They have come together as a single mass, and we call it a hall. It is finished and can be used. Our mind is the same. As long as defilements (kilesa) and craving (taṇhā) are like this – lust, greed, hatred, delusion, ignorance, becoming and birth – it is not yet finished. When they all come together into a single mind...

Ekaṁ cittaṁ – the mind unified as one – then it is accomplished right there.

Ekaṁ dhammaṁ – assembled into one – then it is accomplished in that alone.

That's how it is. If we don't unify, it doesn't get accomplished. There will still be "this is a defilement, that is craving, lust" – it's not finished. Unify it into a single mass. Only then can it be accomplished. This person does not send the mind forward or backward – so who would send it? "My mind isn't still; where does it go?" It doesn't actually go. The light goes. So don't say it goes; if you say it goes, it will go. Don't speak of it. Be calm, be still. Don't send it forward or backward. Just focus and look.

This is attainment: unifying our mind so that it becomes unified. If it unifies, attainment happens right there. If it doesn't unify, there is no attainment. All of you, focus and look. Opanayiko – bring it inward. Paccattaṁ – known only by each one for themselves. This is what people call kamma. Where is kamma?

The Buddha did not teach that kamma lies in the sky, air, mountains, or forests. He said in the Jayamaṅgala Gāthā: kāyakammaṃ vacīkammaṃ manokammaṃ – kamma arises from body, from speech, and from the heart. Kamma is here. It doesn't come from anywhere else. Right now, our bodily kamma is calm. Our verbal kamma has ceased. Only mental kamma remains. Mental kamma is the act of inclining and thinking. Whatever kamma we have thought – whether merit or demerit – that kamma is here. Recall this: Kammassakomhi – kamma is my own possession. Kalyāṇaṃ vā pāpakaṃ vā tassa dāyādā bhavissanti – whatever kamma I have done, whether good or bad, I will inherit its result. We must know this. What is good kamma? In short, it is a calm mind, a good mind, with happiness and comfort. That is called good kamma.

And bad kamma (pāpakaṃ)? What is it? It is an unwholesome mind – suffering, chaotic, distressed. That is bad kamma, and we will receive its results in turn.

When you know that something is not good, abandon it. Here we are, old kamma not yet exhausted, and new kamma keeps adding on. Will it ever run out? Stop it. There is no other way to cleanse oneself of demerit and kamma except by sitting in meditation. When our mind is calm, there is no kamma. Where would kamma come from? I've already told you: it doesn't come from the sky or air. If this person does not create kamma, where would kamma come from? So look into it yourselves. Don't look elsewhere. Look at your own heart. Whoever sees, thinks, or remembers – where does that thinking happen? Bring it right there. Samatha-kammaṭṭhāna and vipassanā-kammaṭṭhāna: samatha means calming.

When the mind is calm, it becomes clear. It can see itself. It sees happiness and suffering. It sees good and evil. It sees that all things are aniccaṁ, dukkhaṁ, anattā – not a self, not a being. Then the mind is calm. When calm, it knows. It gives rise to saṅkhāra and viññāṇa. By contemplating those, one enters vipassanā. Vipassanā is not, as some say, making oneself stiff. It is seeing the arising of saṅkhāra, seeing the ceasing of saṅkhāra, seeing the suffering, the danger, the peril in all saṅkhāra. When you see suffering and danger, you let go. That is vipassanā. Then you cut off everything – all perceptions and mental objects. This is knowing and seeing the truth of the noble truths (sacca-dhamma). See birth, aging, sickness, death. Know that these things are suffering. Why are they suffering? Because of birth, aging, sickness, death. So let's not be born. If we don't arise, what will there be to age? If we don't age, what will there be to get sick? If we don't get sick, what will there be to die? If we don't die, what will there be to suffer? But we are born. Then we age. Then we get sick. Then we die. And after death, we are born again. Then age again, then sick again, then die again. We revolve in birth and death. The Buddha cut the current. He did not arise. Since he did not arise, he did not die. Not dying, he did not suffer. He reached the deathless (amataṁ). Now from here on, each of you listen on your own. Listen to your own heart – not to anything else. Make a mental note: whatever sounds you hear, know that they are not dangerous. You don't need to be upset. Let your mind be at ease, comfortable. Focus and look. Know here whether your mind is good or not good.

If our mind is good, everything we do is good. This is it. People blame this and that. They themselves are not good, yet they blame the country for not being good. They themselves are not peaceful, yet they blame the country for not being peaceful. If each person individually becomes peaceful, then the nation becomes peaceful. Know this well. Each person being peaceful, orderly, and proper – then the community, the marketplace, the whole country becomes peaceful, good, and orderly. You should all know and understand this. If we are good and orderly, others or other nations will not be able to destroy us – as the saying goes, "the earth will swallow them." Let this be known. We must have virtue (sīla) and Dhamma. Sīla is the normalcy of body and mind. Sīla is a cool, calm, happy, at-ease heart – that is called sīla-dhamma. If the mind is chaotic and distressed, it is not sīla nor Dhamma. A troubled, difficult mind is not sīla nor Dhamma. A body that is orderly and a mind that is orderly – that is sīla. Normal body, normal mind – that is the matter of sīla. Having happiness and comfort – that is the matter of sīla. An uncalm, chaotic, distressed mind is not sīla.

Therefore, the Buddha suppressed distress. That's how it is. Where does he suppress it? With what? Distress is burning with the fire of lust (rāga), burning with the fire of greed (lobha), burning with the fire of hatred (dosa). Ādittaṁ jātiyā jarā maraṇena – burning with birth, burning with aging, burning with sickness, burning with death. Now, if we suppress all suffering, then lust, greed, and hatred also cease. Our mind does not arise, does not age, and so it is not distressed. Whatever does not arise does not burn. When there is no more birth, there is no aging – so it does not burn. When there is no aging, there is no sickness and disease – so it does not burn. When these things are gone, there is no death. No death, no suffering. The wandering on in birth and death is suffering, is burning. This is the crucial point. Therefore, you should all understand the practice, the training, the cultivation of your own character. We have been born and have only looked at external work, only studied other things. We have never studied our own conditioned body (saṅkhāra) even once. So now let us study it. Hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin – how can they be 'me' or 'mine'? Contemplate and investigate like this. Our conditioned body is now plagued by this illness and that illness – nothing but diseases.

The Buddha taught: cakkhurogo – disease of the eye; sotarogo – disease of the ear; ghānarogo – disease of the nose; jivhārogo – disease of the tongue; kāyārogo – disease of the body; manorogo – disease of the mind-heart; pādarogo – disease of the feet; kesārogo – disease of the hair; nakharogo – disease of the nails; dantārogo – disease of the teeth; tacorogo – disease of the skin; maṁsarogo – disease of the flesh; nahārurogo – disease of the sinews; aṭṭhirogo – disease of the bones. That's how diseases arise. Therefore, the Buddha taught us to contemplate these things, to investigate them. When our mind is calm, these diseases disappear. If the mind is not calm, they become disease and danger. That's how it is. Contemplate this body. This is the root of the religion. Whoever wants to see Dhamma should contemplate their own conditioned body. When you see hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, and see that these things have no core or substance – that person sees Dhamma. That person studies Dhamma, knows Dhamma, sees Dhamma. Whoever sees Dhamma sees the Buddha. Whoever sees the Buddha will enter parinibbāna, extinguishing suffering in saṃsāra, no longer needing to wander. Those who attained the path and fruit did not study anything else. For example, the five monks of the Pañcavaggiya – the Buddha taught them to contemplate form (rūpa), to contemplate feeling (vedanā), to contemplate perception (saññā), to contemplate formations (saṅkhāra), to contemplate consciousness (viññāṇa). That's all.

When they saw that all these things are impermanent, suffering, and not-self, they attained right there. The mind became calm, it let go. The Buddha asked: Is this conditioned body happiness or suffering? Is it permanent or impermanent? They replied: Impermanent. He asked: That which is impermanent – is it happiness or suffering? They said: Suffering. He asked: That which is suffering – can it be considered a self or belonging to a self? They said: No. Then they let go. Because they let go, they attained.

How did the Buddha become enlightened? He investigated paṭiccasamuppāda – this very thing. He investigated birth, aging, sickness, death – these very four noble truths. Thus he saw birth, aging, and death as noble truths. He didn't contemplate anything else. Now we have also investigated deeply. These four elements – earth, water, fire, air – come together to form the body. We separate them out into water element, earth element, fire element. Then we penetrate to the knower. We know them as elements, see them as elements. The knower doesn't become anything. Then there is no grasping or clinging. One is released and freed. That's how it is. Therefore, investigate deeply until you reach the knower, and you will see these truths for yourselves.
