**13. Three Duties of a Lay Practitioner**

**By Phra Ajahn Funn Ācāro**

**Wat Paa Udomsomphorn, Sakon Nakorn, Thailand**

January 21, 1978


When we were monks, we had many trainings and many duties. Just the thirteen *dhutaṅga* (ascetic practices) alone were there. Now that we have disrobed and become laymen, we have only three duties: *dāna-vatta* – the duty of giving offerings and making merit; *sīla-vatta* – the duty of observing precepts; and *bhāvanā-vatta* – the duty of meditation. Doing these daily as our regular practice – the things we wish for in this world – are just these three duties.

Our aims: firstly, we want to have lots of material possessions, money, and wealth. Secondly, we want beautiful forms and appearances, long life, freedom from disease and danger. Thirdly, we want wisdom and intelligence. That’s all. So we study and learn. Why doesn’t everyone succeed in achieving their aspirations? It’s because we lack the supporting conditions – we lack the habits, i.e., the actual doing of these things. We are lacking those three duties.

Therefore, we must have confidence in the results (*ānisaṃsa*) of what we have done through these duties. For example, if we want material possessions, money, and wealth, we must rely on the *ānisaṃsa* of our own generosity. When we have given gifts and made merit, then in whatever birth or realm we are born in – whether in this life or future lives – that *ānisaṃsa* will reflect back its results, so we obtain wealth, become one who does not starve or suffer want, one who is not poor or in difficulty. This is because we have already deposited it in the Buddha’s Dispensation, buried it within our own minds, because we have done it, we have built it. Those *ānisaṃsa* follow us, like our own shadow follows our body, to whatever birth or realm we go.

The *ānisaṃsa* of observing precepts is the restraint of body, speech, and mind to make them proper. When we no longer commit any wrongdoings, great or small, with body or mind, then in whatever birth or realm we are born, we become people with complete bodies, beautiful and handsome, without disease or danger, without depravity or corruption. Because we have restrained and guarded ourselves – our bodies are proper, our speech is proper, with no faults great or small – then we are complete people. Wherever we go, there is no danger, no enemies, no evil. As laypeople, our basic precepts are the five precepts. From the moment we are born from our mother’s womb, we already have the five precepts with us. What are they? Our two legs – that’s two; our two arms – that makes four; and the head – that’s the fifth. They are all present from birth, namely the five precepts themselves. When we have these five precepts, we abstain from the five faults.

Why do we abstain from the five faults? *Pāṇa* – that is a fault. *Adinna* – that is a fault. *Kāme* – that is a fault. *Musā* – that is a fault. *Surā* – that is a fault. They are all faults. No one wants or desires these five things. The trouble and difficulty we have today are precisely because of these five things – people do not abstain from them. Therefore, we undertake the precepts. The Buddha said: *pāṇā tipi veramaṇī, adinnā tipi veramaṇī, kāmesu micchācārā tipi veramaṇī, musāvādā tipi veramaṇī, surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā tipi veramaṇī*. *Veramaṇī* means to abstain. If we all abstain from these five faults, then we are at ease. Wherever we go, we are at ease. Sitting is easy, sleeping is easy, walking is easy, standing is easy – because we don’t have these five faults. The suffering and difficulty we have now are because of these five faults. Why say that? Because we fear being killed, we fear being robbed, we fear sexual misconduct from others, we fear false speech, we fear intoxicated people. That’s what we fear. If these five things were absent, our towns and our country would prosper.

Because there is no killing – even a red ant or a tiny insect, the Buddha taught not to kill. They love life; we love life. If we don’t do it, why would anyone kill another person? If we don’t have these things, then all those faults disappear. If we kill others, later they will kill us – we will be born with short lives, die young, die in childhood, be separated from loved ones, or contract diseases and dangers. We don’t want that. Since we don’t want it, we should abstain from *pāṇātipāta* (killing). That’s how it is.

Now, as for *adinnādāna* (taking what is not given): wherever we go, we fear thieves. We fear thieves, so we abstain. If everyone abstains, then there will be no thieves. We can leave our belongings anywhere and be at ease. Houses wouldn’t need locks and keys. We don’t desire or want that. If we steal from others, those karmic results follow us, in this life and future lives. Know this well. If we don’t steal, then no such kamma exists. If we do steal, that kamma bears fruit: in whatever birth or realm we are born, there will be only thieves, robbers, plunderers, or we will be punished and fined by authorities, or attacked by bandits, or suffer floods, or be blown away by storms. We don’t want that, we don’t desire it. Therefore, the Buddha taught *adinnādānā veramaṇī* – abstain from taking what is not given.

Now, regarding sexual misconduct (*kāmesu micchācāra*): Why? So that we can have a family, get married, live together, have a wife or husband, and be faithful to each other – then we are at ease. If we have a wife and have to constantly fear that she will go with another man, we don’t want that. Or if we have a husband who goes with another woman, we don’t want that. Think about it. Or if we have children and someone harms them, we don’t want that. Or whoever wants it? It’s ourselves. If we have precepts, and we respect others’ children, wives, and husbands, we don’t harm each other, and we live happily. If we transgress against others, that kamma follows us for many lives and births. We get such children and spouses – husband and wife quarrel, are incompatible. That’s why the Buddha prohibited sexual misconduct. Children who are hard to teach, hard to raise, hard to educate – that’s because of this kamma. Therefore, we are told to abstain from *kāme* (misconduct in sensual pleasures).

Now, people cheat, deceive, or swindle us – we don’t want that. No one wants it. That’s why regarding false speech (*musāvāda*), the Buddha taught to abstain. As for intoxicated and heedless people, we don’t want them either. A drunken husband, a drunken wife, drunken children, drunken parents – we don’t want that. That kamma gives results: one is born an epileptic, born insane or mentally deranged, born deaf, blind, with a twisted mouth, crippled, leprous, afflicted with skin diseases, miserable and destitute. No one wants that, yet no one abstains from alcohol, cannabis, or opium.

If we abstain from these five faults, the Buddha said: *sīlena sugatiṃ yanti* – through virtue one goes to a happy destiny. One has happiness wherever one goes because the five faults are gone. Wherever one stays, one is happy. Sitting, lying, standing, walking are all happy. From now on, abstain from these five things and do not do them. When we abstain, all that kamma ends. If we don’t abstain, it doesn’t end. It becomes karmic accumulation, karmic continuation. The troubles we have now are because of these five faults – we don’t abstain. If we abstain from these five, we are at ease. The country wouldn’t need police, wouldn’t need soldiers, no prisons, no judges. Think about it. Even a red ant or a tiny insect, the Buddha taught not to harm. Even the blood of a mosquito, we shouldn’t take. There would be no petty thieves, no sexual misconduct, no cheating or deceiving, no intoxicated people. Without these five, where would we find any trouble or conflict? There would be none, wouldn’t there? We would be at ease. We gain wealth and happiness because we abstain from these five faults – not because we do other work. The Buddha didn’t say that by trade or business one goes to a happy destiny. He said: *sīlena sugatiṃ yanti* – one has happiness because of observing precepts. *Sīlena bhogasampadā* – one has wealth because of observing precepts. If we have no morality, whatever we do does not prosper. If we have virtue, then we prosper, we have happiness, we are at ease, we have empathy for one another. There is no danger, no enemies, no evil. Whatever we do encounters no obstacles; everything is convenient.

Now, the duty of meditation (*bhāvanā-vatta*): The Buddha taught us to meditate, to go to the temple and listen to Dhamma. Meditation means contemplating and selecting within ourselves, selecting our own minds. The human mind has many states. Therefore, the Buddha taught us to select:

*Manussatiracchāno* – human body but animal mind. What is an animal mind? Lazy, sluggish; after eating it sleeps, does nothing, doesn’t know how to make merit or give gifts, doesn’t know how to pay respect, bow, or venerate anything.

*Manussapeto* – human body but *peta* (hungry ghost) mind. It has only anger and aversion in the mind – that’s a *peta*, isn’t it?

*Manussanirayo* – human body but hell-being mind. What is that like? A mind that is distressed, burning with suffering and torment – hell is like that.

Therefore, the Buddha taught us to meditate, to select and examine our own mind. When we see these things, we stop them, abandon them, let them go. Then we become *manussamanusso* – human body and human mind.

*Manussadevo* – human body but celestial being (*deva*) mind. What is that like? A mind that bows, pays respect, makes merit, gives gifts. A clear, bright mind that meditates, venerates, makes merit, gives gifts.

*Manussabrahmmā* – human body but Brahmā mind. When we observe precepts and meditate, contemplating deeply, the mind becomes completely empty, leaving only the formless consciousness. That mind then becomes Brahmā.

*Manussarahanto* – human body but Arahant mind. Arahants are humans, not anything else. When a human meditates and contemplates, abandoning defilements – lust, greed, hatred, delusion, ignorance, craving, clinging, becoming, birth – free from the influxes (*āsava*), no longer wandering on in birth and death – that is an Arahant.

And then *Manussabuddho* – human body but Buddha mind. What is a Buddha like? It is one who knows and sees through completely all phenomena of every kind, without anyone to instruct or teach him. He awakened by himself, knowing and penetrating fully the 84,000 Dhamma aggregates (*Dhamma-khandha*). The reason he awakened to those Dhammas was precisely through the *ānisaṃsa* of this meditation. He said that these Dhammas are gathered as the collections (*piṭaka*): the *Suttanta Piṭaka*, the *Vinaya Piṭaka*, and the *Paramattha Piṭaka*. These three *Piṭakas* together comprise the 84,000 Dhamma aggregates. All Dhammas arise from this, not from anywhere else. He contemplated using the three kinds of knowledge (*ñāṇa*):

1.  *Pubbenivāsānussati-ñāṇa* – knowledge of recollecting past lives. He contemplated the planes of existence – whatever he had been born as, in whatever realm or plane, he knew completely, knowing successively back and back until no origin could be found. That is why he was courageous and able to establish the Dispensation.

2.  The second knowledge: *Cutūpapāta-ñāṇa* – knowledge of the passing away and arising of beings. When we pass away from here, where will we be reborn? The Buddha knew completely. Whoever had done what, whoever was a *peta*, an animal, a hell-being, a celestial being, a god, or a Brahmā – he knew completely. He taught that this meditation is the path; he taught that this concentration and wisdom (*samādhi-paññā*) is the way of practice.

3.  The third knowledge: *Āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa* – knowledge of the destruction of the influxes. Those who are free from the influxes, defilements, becomings, and births – he knew. The Buddha knew the 84,000 Dhamma aggregates contained in the three *Piṭakas*: the *Suttanta Piṭaka*, *Vinaya Piṭaka*, and *Paramattha Piṭaka*. They are contained within our own body. The *Suttas* are the inhalation of breath; the *Vinaya* is the exhalation of breath; the *Paramattha Piṭaka* is the knowing one inside that preserves the life faculty (*jīvitindriya*) so that it does not break apart. These 84,000 Dhamma aggregates are thus the Three Treasures that protect us. We must practice for ourselves. Who can make us do it? Even our parents cannot make us. It depends on the Dhamma itself. *Dhammo have rakkhati dhammacāriṃ* – Dhamma protects the one who lives by Dhamma. Beings depend on Dhamma, and Dhamma depends on beings. If beings practice Dhamma well, then Dhamma brings them goodness. Think about it: who makes our in-breath and out-breath? If it could be made, then hold your breath and don’t die! If the Three Treasures were to abandon us, no doctor could save us, no one could save us. When our body runs out of breath, it is like a piece of wood, a log – nothing. Therefore, we must have the Three Treasures protecting us. If we practice well, they bring us good; if we practice poorly, they bring us bad.

Thus, the Buddha taught us to have these three duties, to train and cultivate our character. Now, beauty is not the beauty of necklaces and garlands. In the Pali, it is said: *ādikalyāṇaṃ majjhekalyāṇaṃ pariyosānakalyāṇaṃ* – beautiful in the beginning, beautiful in the middle, beautiful in the end. This is true beauty. Beauty in the beginning means having virtue, i.e., restraining body, speech, and mind properly, not committing any fault great or small with body or mind, keeping the mind normal, seeing the normality of our body and mind. When we have normal, proper virtue, then *majjhekalyāṇaṃ* – beauty in the middle is concentration (*samādhi*). With concentration, the mind becomes firmly established, the mind is steady and upright, the mind is cool and calm, the mind is happy and at ease. That is *samādhi*. When it is established, it is steady. People today suffer because they are not steady. *Aniccaṃ* means unsteady. The Buddha taught us to make the mind steady like Nibbāna, which is permanent, not changing or shifting, free from everything. Because we are unsteady, we are like this. Whatever is unsteady, that is suffering. Whatever is suffering, that is not self. Therefore, he taught us to make the mind steady. Then, when the mind is established, *pariyosānakalyāṇaṃ* – beauty in the end is wisdom (*paññā*). We then have wisdom, which gives rise to knowledge. Knowing what? Knowing our own mind, not something else – that is, full comprehension of the aggregate of formations (*saṅkhāra*). *Saṅkhāra* means that which composes and concocts: it concocts into wholesome, unwholesome, or neither-wholesome-nor-unwholesome. It keeps concocting and composing like that. We must fully comprehend it in time. Whatever it concocts or composes, we should consider: does this lead to freedom from suffering or not? Let it know. What is not conducive to freedom from suffering? That which has ill will, vengeance, building up karmic enmity and danger – that does not lead to freedom from suffering. What is not conducive to greed, hatred, and delusion, or is conducive to a calm mind that does not concoct or compose – that indeed leads to freedom from suffering. That is, the mind abandons all evil things, thus escaping from suffering, from faults, from danger, from greed, hatred, and delusion. Therefore, it becomes conducive to our inner happiness and prosperity. Know this.

This is our practice. We must do it constantly. The Buddha taught that virtue, concentration, wisdom are the core of his Dispensation. He did not establish them in trees, mountains, caves, forests, jungles, houses, cities, or roads. He established them in each and every one of us. Virtue is the proper restraint of body, speech, and mind. Concentration is making the mind steady. Wisdom is full comprehension of the aggregate of formations. He established the Dispensation right here. Within us, everything is complete. The 84,000 Dhamma aggregates, having listened, are not anything far away. When gathered in, they are: the *Suttas* – the inhalation of breath; the *Vinaya* – the exhalation of breath; the *Paramattha* – the knowing one inside.

The *Paramattha* is arranged into seven books – the *Abhidhamma* seven books. When we first hear this, it seems far away, but when explained, it is right within ourselves. *Kāmāvacara kusala cittaṃ* – the mind that thinks, that constructs, that concocts – where is it? Everything arises from this mind. Wholesome and unwholesome arise from the mind, not from anywhere else. *Somanassa* – happiness; when our mind delights in wholesome things, it becomes wholesome; when it delights in unwholesome things, it becomes unwholesome. *Ñāṇa* – knowledge; knowledge is not elsewhere; our mind is the knower. Whatever form we take as an object – beautiful form or ugly form – if we take any form as object, we create a state of being there, establish a state of being there. Whatever sound we take as object, whatever smell, whatever taste, whatever tactile sensation, whatever mental object – what is it in our mind like? If the object is good, it goes to *kāmāvacara kusala* (wholesome sensual sphere); if the object is bad, it goes to the four woeful planes. This is the second book. *Pañcakkhandhā* – the five aggregates. *Rūpakkhandha* – the form aggregate is what is sitting here. *Vedanākkhandha* – the feeling aggregate is the experiencing of pleasure and pain: pleasant feeling, painful feeling. *Saññākkhandha* – the perception aggregate: what is that which remembers and marks? *Saṅkhārakkhandha* – the formations aggregate: what is that which concocts and composes? And who is the one that concocts and composes? *Viññāṇakkhandha* – the consciousness aggregate: *viññāṇa* is knowing; *paṭisandhi-viññāṇa* (rebirth-linking consciousness). He taught us to contemplate – we are already doing it. As we have listened: the *indriya* (faculties) – what about them? *Saṅgaho asaṅgaho* – helping others; we help others with merit, so we go toward merit; we help others with evil, so we go toward evil. Whatever way we help, we go that way. If we don’t help, we don’t go. So, when gathered together, the seven books are all within us. This is the practice.

When we have heard and listened, *ehipassiko* – the Dhamma invites us to come and see. He taught to come and see Dhamma, not to go and see Dhamma elsewhere. He taught to come and see our own *rūpa-dhamma* (material phenomena) and *nāma-dhamma* (mental phenomena). The material phenomena of our own body – what is there? We assume we are a person, a woman, a man – where is that exactly? We should look closely. Why do we look closely? In order to abandon clinging to the aggregates (*upādānakkhandha*), to abandon personality view (*sakkāyadiṭṭhi*), to abandon skeptical doubt (*vicikicchā*), to abandon attachment to rites and rituals (*sīlabbataparāmāsa*) – not anything far away. We bring it in, we contemplate the primary meditation subject (*mūla-kammaṭṭhāna*). When we ordained, the teacher taught this *mūla-kammaṭṭhāna*. We think that a *kammaṭṭhāna* monk is one who lives in the forest, but no – everything is *kammaṭṭhāna*. The *mūla-kammaṭṭhāna* teaches: *kesā* – hair. Whose hair? Where is it? Wherever the hair is, right there is the *kammaṭṭhāna*. *Lomā* – body hair – the *kammaṭṭhāna* of body hair. *Nakhā* – nails – the *kammaṭṭhāna* of nails. Where is it? *Dantā* – teeth – the *kammaṭṭhāna* of teeth. Where is it? *Taco* – skin – the *kammaṭṭhāna* of skin. Where is it? It envelops our whole body. These five things – this is the *kammaṭṭhāna*. Why did the Buddha teach the *mūla-kammaṭṭhāna*? Because these are the root cause that deludes beings in this world. Seeing hair, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, they mistake them for a self, for a being, for a person – “me” and “him,” “beautiful” and “handsome.” When we see them like that, we become deluded.

Now, when we contemplate these things and see that they are not a person, they are not beautiful, not handsome, not clean or pure, not good or excellent – when we see like that, our mind withdraws the clinging to aggregates, and we can abandon personality view (*sakkāyadiṭṭhi*). Nowadays we hold that these are beautiful things, that they are a person. What beauty? Hair falls into our rice and food – can we eat it? How is it beautiful? We have to wash and groom it every day. How is it beautiful? Try not washing or grooming it for a month or two. Could you go near anyone? Or what do you say? Body hair and nails – we have to pick and clean them constantly. When they fall into our rice and food, we can’t accept it, right? Teeth – we have to brush them every day, clean them every day. If you don’t clean them for a month, try it. How beautiful, how handsome can they be when they are like that? If a tooth falls into your plate of rice, you throw away the whole plate – you can’t accept it. So how can you say it’s good? We must contemplate to see it like that. And skin – we have to wash it with soap every day. If we don’t wash it for a month, try it. Oh, what is it like? How can you call it beautiful? This is the delusion that makes people so confused. If we see things as they truly are, we abandon personality view. Would anyone accept human skin that fell into their rice? Human skin – they’d throw away the whole plate. So we should know and see this. When we know and see like this, we withdraw clinging to the aggregates. It is not a self, not a being, not a person, not beautiful, not handsome. Our mind can then let go. It abandons personality view. No matter what anyone says or curses, we don’t get angry. There’s nothing – why get angry? So the Buddha taught this to enable us to abandon greed, hatred, and delusion. By contemplating this *mūla-kammaṭṭhāna*, when we see the truth, we withdraw from becomings and births; we no longer wander on in birth and death. If we see this, we know Dhamma. When we know Dhamma and see Dhamma, our mind becomes steady and unshakable.

From now on, you must go to the temple every day to listen to Dhamma. Remember this. Our refuge is *Buddho* – the Buddha; *Dhammo* – the Dhamma; *Saṅgho* – the Noble Sangha. You must pay homage every day. Our refuges are only these – nothing else can be relied upon. Material possessions, money, wealth, buildings, houses – they cannot be relied upon. *Buddho*, *Dhammo*, *Saṅgho* – when condensed, become the single word *Buddho*. If you lack *Buddha* – that is, the knowing one – you cannot rely on anything at all. You cannot rely on *Dhammo* nor *Saṅgho*.

*Buddho* means the knower. Pay homage every day. After paying homage, wherever you go, keep it in mind. You must go to the temple and observe your mind: focus and see “*Buddho, Buddho*.” Examine it every single day. If you don’t examine it – missing a day or two – it won’t do. You must examine it constantly. When sitting, examine; when lying down, examine; when standing, examine; when walking, examine. Why examine? So that you know. When you examine, you see: “Ah, our mind is good. Sitting is good, standing is good, walking is good, going anywhere is good – no danger, no enemies, no evil, no harm.” That is good. If we don’t examine, we don’t know. It’s dark like the night – we can’t see ourselves, can we? Others can’t see us; the trees, the ground – we can’t see them; it’s pitch black. But if we examine, it’s like lighting a lamp, or like daytime. We see ourselves, see other people, see all living beings; we see our own life; we see our own suffering and happiness. We were born and we love our life, love ourselves, love what is ours. If that is so, when others harm us – do we feel good? No, we don’t. And others – when we harm them, do they feel good? They don’t either.

Since both sides don’t feel good, we should not harm each other. Likewise, if a thief steals from us, we don’t feel good; if we steal from him, he doesn’t feel good. If he kills us, we don’t feel good; if we kill him, he doesn’t feel good. Since everyone loves their own life like this, then we should not harm one another. That is why we are taught to go to the temple. Nowadays, people don’t examine themselves at all. They are completely in the dark. Therefore, they kill, slash, and stab each other without thinking of their own selves. Their own selves fear death; others also fear death. So don’t do it. If everyone knew this and practiced this, everyone would be at ease, the town would be peaceful, the country would be peaceful, the human world would be peaceful. Peace is happiness. There is no happiness equal to peace.

Therefore, from now on, perform this regular practice: go to the temple every day. Go to the temple and listen to Dhamma every day. *Buddho, Dhammo, Saṅgho* – our refuges are only these. Nothing else can be relied upon at all. If you know this, remember it. Go to the temple every day. Contemplate yourself so that you see within. See what is good, what is bad, what is happiness, what is suffering. When you see, doubt ends, and you have only confidence in the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha. Then you have a refuge, a shelter. There is nothing in the world like it.
